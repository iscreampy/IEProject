Native Plants vs Non-Native Plants

Native plants grow naturally in a given region or ecosystem. They establish a symbiotic relationship with the native wildlife over thousands of years hence creating a sustainable habitat.

  • Native plants can survive on rainfall alone!
  • They provide habitat, shelter and food for local wildlife.
  • They improve biodiversity.

Non-native plants are plants introduced to a new environment. They may originate from different regions of the same state, different states of the same country or different countries. They are also know as Alien Species.

Non-native species can't all be that bad. Can they?

In order to answer that question we will first have to answer three questions that are pertinent to human existence : Environment, Money & Food.

  • 01 Do non-native species pose a threat to the bio-diversity?

    Quickly multiplying invasive plants displace native plants resulting in reduced plant species diversity, monocultures & cause a disruption to the environment.

  • 02 Are these invasive species causing economic losses?

    Yes! The annual combined cost of invasive species (management, control etc.) is estimated to be more than 13.6 billion$ (AUD).

  • 03 Do these species contribute to food shortages?

    True. Invasive plant species disrupt water flow, nutrient cycling and soil decomposition causing contamination and destruction to crops leading to food shortages.

 

What is biodiversity?

Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth at all its levels, from genes to ecosystems. It can include evolutionary, ecological, and cultural processes that sustain life.

How does a native garden help with bio-diversity?

We can restore natural habitats by growing a native garden in our backyards. This way we can help sustain micro-ecosystems around us which consist of plants, insects and birds. These networks form an integral part of our ecosystem services like pest regulation, climate regulation and pollination.

How do Native plants help our ecosystem?

Natural insects have developed a special ability to digest the leaves of natural host plants that have co-evolved with them. Therefore, they cannot feed on most non-native plants.

  • No Native trees means no insects, then no birds...
  • Thousands of caterpillars are need to feed a litter of young birds.
  • The chicks of most birds feed on insect larvae.

What does it mean to plant native trees & shrubs?

Growing native plants means encouraging local insect life which feeds the local birds and other smaller animals that feed on the insects. As we know, insects help in pollination which means that healthy insect population means healthy food production.

Native Gardening 101

Dig a hole

Dig a hole 2 times the width and height of the plot or seedling container. Loosen the soil around the hole for the roots to push through easily.

Water the hole

Fill the hole with water and let it soak into the ground.

Remove the seedling and hydrate it.

Gently tap the container or pot to release the plant. Soak the plant in water to hydrate it. Place your plant into the hole and backfilll the hole with soil

Place plant into hole and water the plant.

Press the soil to firmly pack it but do not compact the soil. Create a small dip around the plant to capture rainwater. Water the plant immediately to reduce evaporation, settle the soil.

Frequently Asked Questions

Here are some of the questions that you might have during your journey of growing native plants. If you have further queries, feel free to email us.

  • What is the best time to plant native Australian plants?

    Native plants can be planted throughout the year, however, Autumn is generally the best time as it is losses extreme in terms of temperature and soil dryness. Come winter, the plants get sufficient moisture for root growth. This allows the plant to establish itself before the next summer. For winter temperatures below 2 degree Celcius, it is best to plant in Spring after the cold has lessened.

  • Is it necessary to prune my plants? When should I prune?

    Yes. Pruning promotes healthy new growth. It helps maintain the shape of a plant, keeping it compact and dense. This is important for controlling pests, diseases and attracting birds. The best time to prune is after the flowering/ fruiting season is over.

  • Can my backyard garden actually make an impact on the environment?

    Yes, certainly! Plants in your backyard release oxygen to the environment and contribute to cleaner air. The native vegetables and plants in your garden can help cut carbon footprint by reducing your trip to the local grocer. They also provide habitat for local insects, birds and animals.

  • I have a frog pool in my backyard but they frogs have not arrived. Can I bring animals from elsewhere?

    No, catching frogs or tadpoles from local water sources is illegal and can land you in trouble. Your native frogs are really good at finding suitable habitat and they will come to your pond eventually. Just be patient.

  • Can I feed the animals that come to my native habitat garden?

    Do not feed the animals as you may end up feeding them the wrong thing. Your feeding could also cause unhealthy dependence on humans.
